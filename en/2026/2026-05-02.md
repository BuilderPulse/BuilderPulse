**🔍 Signal**: [WhatCable](https://github.com/darrylmorley/whatcable) led Show HN with 128 comments, followed by [GhostBox](https://www.ghost.charity/) with 69, [Perfect Bluetooth MIDI for Windows](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47972888) with 31, [Winpodx](https://github.com/kernalix7/winpodx) with 47, and [Pu.sh](https://pu.dev/) with 26.

**In plain English:** Small utilities win when they explain one hidden machine behavior better than the official label does.

The strongest launch pattern is not broad AI. It is "show me what is actually happening." WhatCable is a tiny macOS menu bar and command-line app for inspecting USB-C cable identity. That sounds small until @jareds says a visual $16 USB tester is not usable for a blind buyer, and @n3storm asks whether Linux can get the same inspection through `lsusb`-style tooling. @billyhoffman also noted the unusually fast response loop: @sleepingNomad shipped 16 releases in roughly seven hours after HN feedback.

Perfect Bluetooth MIDI for Windows and Winpodx are classic "one awkward platform gap" tools. One fixes BLE-MIDI exposure through Windows APIs that many digital audio workstations do not poll; the other tries to run Windows apps on Linux as native windows. These are not glamorous markets, but they have clear users and visible frustration.

**Takeaway**: Ship a narrow diagnostic utility before a broad assistant; today's launches reward tools that translate hidden system state into one clear decision.

**Counter-view**: Many of these products are small enough to become free features or weekend curiosities rather than lasting businesses.
